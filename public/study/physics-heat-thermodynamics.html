🌐The Big Picture

Heat is energy on the move. Follow it: it raises temperature, expands matter, changes states, and travels three ways.
Heat = energy in transit→ Expands / heats matter→ Changes state (latent)→ Travels: conduct/convect/radiate
Anchor ideaHeat and temperature are not the same. Heat is the energy that flows from hot to cold; temperature is how hot something is. A spark at 1000°C carries far less heat than a bucket of warm water — high temperature, little energy.

🌡️1 · Heat vs Temperature

What
Heat = energy transferred because of a temperature difference (joules). Temperature = degree of hotness / average molecular kinetic energy (kelvin, °C).
Why the difference matters
A tiny spark is at a very high temperature but carries little heat; a warm bathtub is at a lower temperature but holds huge heat. Amount of energy (heat) ≠ intensity (temperature).
How
Heat always flows hot → cold until both reach the same temperature (thermal equilibrium) — the idea behind every thermometer.
Where
Cooking, thermometers, why metal feels colder than wood at the same temperature.
Exam
"Heat = energy (joule); temperature = degree of hotness (kelvin)." "Heat flows hot → cold." 1 calorie = 4.18 J.
Why metal feels colder than woodBoth are at room temperature, but metal conducts heat away from your hand quickly, so it feels cold. Wood conducts poorly, so it feels warmer — a sensation of heat flow, not of temperature.

↔️2 · Thermal Expansion

What
Most substances expand on heating and contract on cooling. Gases expand most, then liquids, then solids.
Why
Heating gives molecules more energy, so they vibrate harder and push farther apart, increasing the size.
How
Water is anomalous: from 0→4°C it contracts, then expands. It is densest at 4°C — a life-saving exception.
Where
Rail/bridge gaps, sagging overhead wires in summer, bimetallic strips in thermostats and fire alarms, rivets, hot glass cracking.
Exam
"Water densest at 4°C." "Gaps in rails → expansion." "Bimetallic strip → thermostat." Gas expands most.
Why fish survive a frozen lakeBecause water is densest at 4°C, the coldest 4°C water sinks to the bottom while ice forms on top. The ice layer insulates the water below, so fish live in liquid water even when the surface is frozen.

♨️3 · Specific Heat Capacity

What
Specific heat (c) = the heat needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg by 1°C. Heat gained/lost: Q = mcΔT.
Why water is special
Water has an unusually high specific heat (4200 J/kg·K) — it soaks up a lot of heat for a small temperature rise, so it heats and cools slowly.
How
A high-c substance changes temperature slowly; a low-c one (like a metal) heats up and cools down fast.
Where
Water as an engine/radiator coolant, hot-water bottles, and the sea moderating coastal climate.
Exam
"Q = mcΔT." "Water has high specific heat → good coolant, moderate coastal climate." Unit of c = J/kg·K.
Why coastal cities are mildThe sea's high specific heat lets it store enormous heat, warming the coast in winter and cooling it in summer. That's why coastal climates are moderate while deserts (dry land, low heat capacity) swing between hot days and cold nights.

🧊4 · Latent Heat & Change of State

What
Latent heat is the heat absorbed or released during a change of state (melting, boiling) — at constant temperature.
Why temperature stays constant
During melting/boiling, the incoming heat goes into breaking the bonds between molecules, not into speeding them up — so the thermometer holds steady until the change is complete.
How
Latent heat of fusion (ice→water: 336 kJ/kg). Latent heat of vaporization (water→steam: 2260 kJ/kg). Q = mL.
Where
Ice cooling drinks, steam burns worse than boiling water, sweating, earthen pots, desert coolers.
Exam
"Temperature constant during melting/boiling." "Steam burns are severe (extra latent heat)." "Evaporation cools (absorbs latent heat)."
Why a steam burn is worseSteam at 100°C carries a huge extra latent heat of vaporization. When it condenses on skin it dumps that energy plus the heat of the hot water — far more than boiling water at the same temperature.

➡️5 · Heat Transfer

What
Heat moves three ways: conduction (through solids), convection (through moving fluids), radiation (as waves, no medium).
Why three modes
Solids can't flow, so heat passes molecule-to-molecule (conduction). Fluids can carry heat by flowing (convection). Across empty space, only electromagnetic radiation works — that's how the Sun heats Earth.
How
Metals conduct well; air/wool/wood insulate. Dull black surfaces absorb & emit radiation best; shiny surfaces reflect.
Where
Cooking pans (conduction), sea/land breezes and boiling (convection), Sun's heat and a fire's warmth (radiation), thermos flask (blocks all three).
Exam
"Sun's heat → radiation (no medium)." "Convection needs a fluid." "Black surfaces absorb/emit best." "Thermos blocks all three."

🎈6 · Gas Laws

What
Three laws link a gas's pressure, volume and temperature: Boyle's, Charles's and the pressure (Gay-Lussac) law.
Why
Gas molecules fly around and collide with the walls. Squeeze them (less volume) and they hit more often (more pressure); heat them and they move faster (more pressure or volume).
How
Boyle: PV = constant (const T). Charles: V ∝ T (const P). Pressure law: P ∝ T (const V). Temperatures must be in kelvin.
Where
A syringe, a balloon shrinking in the cold, a pressure cooker, a deodorant can warning "do not heat".
Exam
"Boyle: P ∝ 1/V." "Charles: V ∝ T." "Sealed can bursts on heating → pressure law." "Absolute zero = −273°C = 0 K."

⚗️7 · Calorimetry & Thermometry

What
Calorimetry measures heat exchange; thermometry measures temperature. Scales: Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin.
Why heat lost = heat gained
Energy is conserved. When a hot body and a cold body mix, all the heat the hot one loses is exactly the heat the cold one gains — nothing vanishes.
How
K = °C + 273; °F = (9/5)°C + 32. Water: 0°C = 32°F = 273 K; 100°C = 212°F = 373 K. −40° is the same on °C and °F.
Where
Clinical thermometers (35–42°C), lab calorimeters, pyrometers for furnaces, weather reports.
Exam
"Heat lost = heat gained." "K = °C + 273." "37°C = 310 K = 98.6°F." "−40°C = −40°F."

🧠Memory Engine

One hook per recall-worthy fact. Concept → hook → recall question.
Heat vs Temperature peg
"Heat is the money, temperature is the price tag." A spark has a high tag (temp) but little money (heat); a bathtub is the reverse.
Q: Which is measured in joules? → Heat.
Water densest at 4°C number peg
"Four for the floor." The densest water (4°C) sinks to the bottom — so fish survive under ice.
Q: Temperature of maximum water density? → 4°C.
Q = mcΔT chant
"Mass × c × delta-T." For a temperature change use mcΔT; for a state change use mL (no ΔT).
Q: Heat to warm 2 kg water by 10°C (c=4200)? → 84000 J.
Water's big appetite why-hook
Water has a huge specific heat (4200) — it's a slow eater and slow cooler, so it makes a great coolant and mild coasts.
Q: Why is water a good coolant? → High specific heat.
Latent = hidden heat word hook
"Latent means hidden." During melting/boiling the heat hides in breaking bonds — the thermometer doesn't move.
Q: What happens to temp during boiling? → Stays constant.
Steam burns worse why-hook
"Steam packs extra punch." It dumps the latent heat of vaporization (2260 kJ/kg) on top of the hot water's heat.
Q: Why is a steam burn severe? → Extra latent heat.
Evaporation cools everyday
"Sweat steals heat." Evaporating water grabs latent heat from your skin — cooling you. Same for a matka and a desert cooler.
Q: Why do we feel cold after a bath? → Evaporation absorbs latent heat.
Three heat modes mnemonic
"Conduction (solids), Convection (currents/fluids), Radiation (rays, no medium)." Sun's heat = radiation.
Q: How does the Sun's heat reach us? → Radiation.
Black absorbs, shiny reflects visual
"Dull black drinks heat; shiny mirrors it away." Wear white in summer; a thermos is silvered inside.
Q: Best absorber of radiation? → Dull black surface.
Gas laws pairing chant
"Boyle P-V (temp fixed); Charles V-T (pressure fixed)." Boyle squeezes, Charles heats.
Q: P ∝ 1/V is whose law? → Boyle's.
Temperature conversions peg
"+273 to Kelvin; ×9/5 +32 to Fahrenheit." And the magic point: −40° is the same on both °C and °F.
Q: 37°C in Kelvin? → 310 K.

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Each has been set in real papers. Read the reality line and it dies.
Trap 1 · Heat ≠ TemperatureHeat is energy (joule); temperature is the degree of hotness (kelvin). A high temperature doesn't mean a lot of heat.
Trap 2 · Water densest at 4°C, not 0°CWater is densest at 4°C (anomalous expansion). "Densest at 0°C" is false.
Trap 3 · Ice is less dense than waterIce floats because it is less dense than water. So the surface freezes first while fish live below.
Trap 4 · Temperature is constant during a state changeWhile ice melts or water boils, the temperature does not rise — the heat becomes latent heat.
Trap 5 · Radiation needs no mediumRadiation travels through a vacuum (Sun → Earth). Only conduction and convection need a medium.
Trap 6 · Convection needs a fluidConvection happens in liquids and gases, never in solids (solids use conduction).
Trap 7 · Black absorbs, not reflectsDull black surfaces are the best absorbers and emitters of radiation. Shiny/white surfaces reflect it.
Trap 8 · Steam vs boiling waterSteam at 100°C causes worse burns than water at 100°C because it releases extra latent heat on condensing.
Trap 9 · Absolute zeroAbsolute zero = −273.15°C = 0 K, not 0°C. Gas-law temperatures must always be in kelvin.
Trap 10 · Boyle vs CharlesBoyle's law fixes temperature (P ∝ 1/V); Charles's law fixes pressure (V ∝ T). Don't swap them.
Trap 11 · Q = mcΔT vs Q = mLUse mcΔT only when temperature changes; use mL during a state change (no ΔT).
Trap 12 · Water's specific heat is HIGHWater has a high specific heat (~4200 J/kg·K), which is why it heats/cools slowly. "Low specific heat" is false.
Assertion–ReasonA: Ponds freeze at the top first. R: Water is densest at 4°C, so the coldest water sinks and ice forms on top. → Both true, R explains A.
Assertion–ReasonA: We can cool a drink faster with ice than with cold water. R: Melting ice absorbs latent heat of fusion. → Both true, R explains A.
