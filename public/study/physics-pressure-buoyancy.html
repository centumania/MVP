🌐The Big Picture

First: how a force spread over an area becomes pressure. Then: how fluids press, transmit and push up on things.
Force ÷ area = Pressure→ Fluids press (hρg)→ Pascal transmits it→ Buoyancy lifts (Archimedes)
Anchor ideaThe same force feels completely different depending on the area it acts over. A drawing pin barely pushed pierces the wall because its tiny tip concentrates the force into enormous pressure. Fluids add a twist: they press in all directions and push up on anything inside them.

📌1 · Thrust & Pressure

What
Thrust is the force acting perpendicular to a surface. Pressure = thrust ÷ area (P = F/A).
Why area matters
Force stays the same, but sharing it over a smaller area concentrates it into higher pressure. That's why a sharp edge cuts and a broad foot doesn't sink.
How
Unit is the pascal (1 Pa = 1 N/m²). Small area → high pressure; large area → low pressure.
Where
Knives, needles, nails (sharp); camel feet, tractor/tank tracks, snowshoes, foundations (broad).
Exam
"Why is a knife sharp?" → small area, more pressure. "Why broad camel feet / wide tank tracks?" → large area, less pressure. Unit = pascal.
🔪 Knife

Thin edge → high pressure → cuts

🐫 Camel feet

Broad → low pressure → no sinking

🎒 Wide straps

Spread load → comfortable

🏗️ Wide foundations

Low pressure on soil

💧2 · Pressure in Fluids

What
A liquid at rest exerts pressure P = hρg that grows with depth and acts equally in all directions.
Why depth matters
Deeper down, there is more liquid above pressing down, so the pressure is greater. The pressure depends only on height of liquid, density and g — not on the container's shape or width.
How
At the same horizontal level the pressure is equal. This "hydrostatic paradox" means oddly-shaped and narrow vessels give the same base pressure at equal depth.
Where
Dam walls thick at the base, deep-sea diving suits, water towers placed high for good taps, submarine hull design.
Exam
"P = hρg." "Pressure depends on depth & density, not shape/area." "Dam thicker at bottom → pressure rises with depth."
Why water towers are tallPressure = hρg, so the height of the stored water sets the pressure at your tap. A tank on a high tower gives a strong flow to homes far below — no pump needed.

🌬️3 · Atmospheric Pressure

What
Air has weight, so the atmosphere presses on everything with about 101 kPa (76 cm of mercury) at sea level.
Why we don't feel it
The pressure is balanced — air pushes on us from all sides and our body pushes back equally, so we don't notice this ~1 kg per cm² of push.
How
Measured by a barometer. It falls with altitude (less air above) — a falling barometer signals a coming storm.
Where
Drinking straws, syringes, rubber suckers, suction/lift pumps, why cooking takes longer on mountains.
Exam
"1 atm ≈ 76 cm Hg ≈ 101 kPa." "Barometer measures atmospheric pressure." "Straw/sucker → atmospheric pressure." "Pressure decreases with altitude."
Everyday — the strawYou don't "pull" the drink up. You lower the air pressure inside the straw by sucking; the atmosphere pushes the liquid up into the low-pressure space. A rubber sucker sticks the same way.

🛢️4 · Pascal's Law

What
Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished to every part of the fluid and the container walls.
Why it multiplies force
Since pressure is the same everywhere (F/A = constant), a small force on a small piston creates the same pressure that acts over a large piston — producing a much bigger force there.
How
F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂. The bigger the area ratio, the greater the force multiplication (but the big piston moves a shorter distance — work is conserved).
Where
Hydraulic press, hydraulic lift/jack, JCB excavator arms, barber's chair, and car hydraulic brakes.
Exam
"Hydraulic brakes/press → Pascal's law." "F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂." Note a barometer does NOT use Pascal's law (it measures atmospheric pressure).
Why one foot stops a carA hydraulic brake takes the small push of your foot and, through Pascal's law, transmits it as a large, equal braking force to all four wheels at once.

⬆️5 · Buoyancy (Upthrust)

What
Buoyancy (upthrust) is the upward force a fluid exerts on an immersed body, making it feel lighter.
Why it exists
Fluid pressure increases with depth, so the bottom of a submerged object is pushed up harder than the top is pushed down. That difference is a net upward force.
How
Apparent weight = real weight − upthrust. Upthrust depends on the volume submerged and the fluid's density — not on the body's own weight.
Where
Lifting a bucket from a well (easy underwater, heavy once it clears the surface), swimming, floating logs.
Exam
"Body feels lighter in water → upthrust." "Loss of weight in fluid = upthrust = weight of displaced fluid." Upthrust depends on displaced fluid, not the body's weight.

👑6 · Archimedes' Principle

What
A body wholly or partly immersed in a fluid experiences an upthrust equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces.
Why it's so useful
It links a hard-to-measure force (buoyancy) to an easy one (weight of displaced fluid), letting us find density, purity and volume just by weighing in air and water.
How
Loss of weight in water = weight of water displaced = upthrust. This gives relative density = weight in air ÷ loss of weight in water.
Where
Ships, submarines, hot-air balloons, hydrometers, lactometers, and the famous gold-crown test.
Exam
"Upthrust = weight of displaced fluid." "Hydrometer/lactometer → Archimedes." Eureka story = Archimedes.
The 'Eureka!' storyAsked to test a king's crown for pure gold without melting it, Archimedes realised in his bath that a submerged body displaces its own volume of water — letting him compare the crown's density to pure gold.

🚢7 · Floatation & Density

What
Law of floatation: a floating body displaces a weight of fluid exactly equal to its own weight. It floats if its density is less than the fluid's.
Why iron ships float
Iron is denser than water, but a ship is mostly hollow — its huge shape displaces enough water that its average density falls below water's, so it floats.
How
Relative density = density of substance ÷ density of water (no unit). Fraction submerged = ρ_body ÷ ρ_fluid.
Where
Ships, submarines (ballast tanks), icebergs, floating in the salty Dead Sea, a hydrometer bobbing in liquid.
Exam
"Float if density < fluid." "Iceberg: ~1/10 above water." "Relative density has no unit." "Easier to swim in the sea (denser)."
Why ~9/10 of an iceberg hidesIce density ≈ 0.9× water's, so it must sink until it displaces its own weight — about nine-tenths of it ends up below the surface, leaving only a tenth showing. Hence "the tip of the iceberg."

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Each has been set in real papers. Read the reality line and it dies.
Trap 1 · Thrust vs PressureThrust is a force (newtons); pressure is force per area (pascals). They are not the same.
Trap 2 · Sharp = more pressureA sharp knife has a smaller area, giving higher pressure — that's why it cuts. Not "less pressure".
Trap 3 · Fluid pressure & shapeLiquid pressure depends on depth and density, NOT on the container's shape, width or the amount of liquid.
Trap 4 · Pressure acts in all directionsFluid pressure at a point acts equally in all directions, not just downward.
Trap 5 · Atmospheric pressure & altitudePressure decreases with altitude (less air above). It does not increase.
Trap 6 · What a barometer doesA barometer measures atmospheric pressure. It does NOT work on Pascal's law (that's hydraulic devices).
Trap 7 · Upthrust depends on displaced fluidBuoyant force depends on the weight of fluid displaced (volume submerged × fluid density), NOT on the body's own weight.
Trap 8 · Float conditionA body floats if it is less dense than the fluid. "Floats if denser than the fluid" is false.
Trap 9 · Ice is less dense than waterIce floats because it is LESS dense than water (~0.9). "Ice is denser than water" is a classic false statement.
Trap 10 · Relative density has no unitRelative density (specific gravity) is a ratio of two densities → dimensionless. Density (kg/m³) does have a unit.
Trap 11 · Iron ship vs nailBoth are iron. The ship floats because its hollow shape lowers its average density below water's — not because of a different metal.
Trap 12 · Ship river → seaEntering the denser sea, a ship gets more upthrust and rises slightly (floats higher), it does not sink.
Assertion–ReasonA: A stone feels lighter in water. R: Water exerts an upthrust equal to the weight of water displaced. → Both true, R explains A.
Assertion–ReasonA: A hydraulic jack lifts a car with a small effort. R: Pressure in an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally. → Both true, R explains A.
