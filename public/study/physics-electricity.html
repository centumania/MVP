🌐The Big Picture

Charge flows (current), pushed by voltage, opposed by resistance. Ohm's law ties them together; series/parallel combine them; power & safety finish the story.
Charge flows = Current→ V = IR (Ohm)→ Series & Parallel→ Power & Household safety
Anchor ideaThink of a circuit like water in pipes: voltage is the pump pressure, current is the flow rate, and resistance is a narrow pipe. Ohm's law V = IR is just "more pressure → more flow, more narrowing → less flow." Every calculation here is a rearrangement of that.

⚡1 · Charge & Current

What
Current is the rate of flow of charge: I = Q/t. Charge is carried by electrons; its unit is the coulomb.
Why "conventional" current is backwards
Scientists defined current direction (+ to −) before electrons were discovered. Electrons actually flow − to +, but we keep the old convention — a favourite exam trap.
How
1 ampere = 1 coulomb/second. Q = It. The ampere is a base SI unit; charge on one electron = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C.
Where
Every wire, battery and appliance; an ammeter (connected in series) measures current.
Exam
"Current = rate of flow of charge (I = Q/t)." "Unit = ampere." "Conventional current: + to −." Ammeter goes in series.

📏2 · Ohm's Law

What
At constant temperature, the current through a conductor is proportional to the voltage across it: V = IR.
Why "at constant temperature"
Heating a wire changes its resistance, breaking the neat proportionality. So Ohm's law holds only while temperature is steady — that's why a bulb's glowing filament is non-ohmic.
How
Use the triangle: V = IR, I = V/R, R = V/I. An ohmic conductor's V–I graph is a straight line through the origin; its slope is R.
Where
Designing any circuit; choosing resistors; every numerical in this chapter.
Exam
"V = IR." "V–I graph is a straight line for an ohmic conductor." "Bulbs/diodes are non-ohmic."
Ohmic conductor: I vs V is a straight line through the origin.

🚧3 · Resistance

What
Resistance opposes current flow. Unit: ohm (Ω), where 1 Ω = 1 V/A. R = V/I.
Why length & area matter
A longer wire makes electrons collide more → more resistance. A thicker wire (bigger area) gives them more room → less resistance. Like water: a long thin pipe resists flow.
How
R = ρL/A. R ∝ length, R ∝ 1/area, and depends on the material (resistivity ρ) and temperature (rises with temp for metals).
Where
Thin high-resistance heater elements, thick low-resistance power cables, rheostats/dimmers.
Exam
"R = ρL/A." "R increases with length, decreases with area." "Silver is the best conductor (lowest ρ)." Resistivity unit = Ω·m.
Length vs AreaResistance goes up with length but down with cross-sectional area. Options that reverse either relationship are wrong.

➖4 · Series Circuits

What
Components joined end to end in one path. The same current flows through all of them.
Why resistances add
The current must pass through every resistor in turn, so their obstructions stack up: total resistance = R₁ + R₂ + R₃…, always more than any single resistor.
How
Current is common; the voltage divides across the resistors in proportion to their values.
Where
Switches with a device, old-style fairy lights, a fuse in series with an appliance.
Exam
"Series: same current, R = R₁+R₂." "Voltage divides." "One fails → all stop." Good for a control switch.

⚎5 · Parallel Circuits

What
Components connected across the same two points. Each branch has the same voltage.
Why total resistance drops
Adding another branch gives the current an extra path, so more current flows overall — meaning less total resistance (always below the smallest branch).
How
1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ +… The current divides between branches — more goes through the lower-resistance path.
Where
Household wiring — every appliance gets the full 230 V and switches on/off independently.
Exam
"Parallel: same voltage, 1/R = 1/R₁+1/R₂." "Total < smallest branch." "Homes are wired in parallel."
Why homes use parallel wiringIn parallel, each appliance gets the full voltage and can be switched on or off without affecting the others. If homes used series wiring, switching off one light would kill the whole house — and every device would share (and starve on) one current.

💡6 · Electric Power & Heating

What
Power = rate of using electrical energy: P = VI = I²R = V²/R. Unit: watt.
Why current causes heat
Electrons colliding with the wire's atoms lose energy as heat — the heating effect, H = I²Rt (Joule's law). It's the basis of heaters, and the reason bulbs and cables warm up.
How
Energy = power × time. 1 kWh ('unit') = 1000 W for 1 hour = 3.6×10⁶ J — what your electricity bill charges for.
Where
Heaters, irons, geysers, toasters (nichrome); bulbs (tungsten); the monthly electricity bill.
Exam
"P = VI = I²R = V²/R." "H = I²Rt." "1 kWh = 3.6×10⁶ J." Heater element = nichrome; bulb = tungsten.

🏠7 · Household Electricity & Safety

What
Homes run on 230 V AC (50 Hz) parallel wiring, protected by fuses, MCBs and earthing.
Why a fuse saves the house
If too much current flows (overload or short circuit), the thin fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit before the wiring overheats and starts a fire.
How
Live (red/brown) carries current in, neutral (black/blue) returns it, earth (green) is safety. The fuse goes in the live wire; earthing connects the metal body to ground.
Where
Every plug, switchboard, appliance and MCB in the house.
Exam
"Fuse in the live wire." "Earthing → safety path for leakage." "Short circuit: live touches neutral → huge current." Colours: live red, neutral black, earth green.
Everyday — why appliances are earthedIf a fault makes a metal casing 'live', touching it could shock you. The earth wire gives that leakage current a much easier path to the ground, so it flows away safely (and usually blows the fuse) instead of through your body.

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Each has been set in real papers. Read the reality line and it dies.
Trap 1 · Current directionConventional current flows + to −; electrons flow − to +. They are opposite. Don't say electrons flow + to −.
Trap 2 · Ammeter series, Voltmeter parallelAn ammeter is connected in series; a voltmeter in parallel. Reversing these is a top trap.
Trap 3 · Resistance: length vs areaR increases with length, decreases with cross-sectional area. Not the other way round.
Trap 4 · Series total vs Parallel totalSeries resistance adds up (bigger). Parallel resistance is less than the smallest branch — never larger.
Trap 5 · Homes are wired in PARALLELHousehold appliances are in parallel (full voltage, independent). "Homes are wired in series" is false.
Trap 6 · Watt vs kWhWatt is power; kilowatt-hour is energy. "The watt is a unit of energy" is false.
Trap 7 · Fuse in the live wireThe fuse (and switch) go in the live wire, not the neutral or earth.
Trap 8 · Earth wire colourEarth = green (or green-yellow); live = red/brown; neutral = black/blue.
Trap 9 · Bulb vs Heater metalBulb filament = tungsten; heater element = nichrome. Don't swap them.
Trap 10 · Best conductorSilver is the best conductor (lowest resistivity); copper is used widely because it is cheaper.
Trap 11 · Ohmic vs non-ohmicA bulb's glowing filament is non-ohmic (its V–I graph curves). Ohm's law needs constant temperature.
Trap 12 · Short circuit vs overloadA short circuit is live directly touching neutral (huge current); an overload is too many appliances. Both trip the fuse/MCB.
Assertion–ReasonA: Appliances at home are connected in parallel. R: Each then receives the full supply voltage and works independently. → Both true, R explains A.
Assertion–ReasonA: A fuse protects household wiring. R: It melts and breaks the circuit when the current exceeds a safe value. → Both true, R explains A.
