🌐The Big Picture

Electricity and magnetism are two sides of one coin. Trace the loop: current makes magnetism (motor), and magnetism makes current (generator).
Current → magnetic field→ Field pushes a wire (motor)→ Moving field → current (induction)→ Generator
Anchor ideaTwo experiments unlock this whole chapter. Oersted: a current creates a magnetic field. Faraday: a changing magnetic field creates a current. A motor uses the first (electricity → motion); a generator uses the second (motion → electricity). They are mirror images.

🧲1 · Magnets

What
A magnet has two poles (N & S). Like poles repel, unlike attract. It has a magnetic field shown by field lines.
Why poles come in pairs
Magnetism arises from tiny atomic current loops, each with its own N and S. Break a magnet and each piece grows a new pair — you can never isolate a single pole.
How
Field lines run N → S outside the magnet (S → N inside), never cross, and are closer where the field is stronger.
Where
Compasses, fridge magnets, the Earth's own magnetic field, magnetic door catches.
Exam
"Like poles repel." "Field lines N→S outside." "Field lines never cross." Magnetic materials: iron, cobalt, nickel. Earth = a giant magnet.
Field-line rulesLines go N→S outside, they never intersect, and denser lines mean a stronger field. A compass needle sits along a field line.

🔄2 · Magnetic Field of a Current

What
A current-carrying wire produces a magnetic field around it — discovered by Oersted (1820) when a compass deflected near a wire.
Why it mattered
It was the first proof that electricity and magnetism are connected — the seed of motors, generators and all modern electrical technology.
How
Right-hand thumb rule: grip the wire with the right hand, thumb pointing along the current — the curled fingers show the field direction (concentric circles).
Where
Every wire; the basis of electromagnets, relays and solenoids.
Exam
"Oersted linked electricity & magnetism." "Field around a straight wire = concentric circles." "Right-hand thumb rule gives the field direction."

🌀3 · Solenoid & Electromagnet

What
A solenoid is a long current-carrying coil. Its field is like a bar magnet. Add a soft-iron core → a strong electromagnet.
Why soft iron
Soft iron magnetises strongly with the current but loses its magnetism the instant the current stops — perfect for a switchable magnet. Steel would stay magnetised (used for permanent magnets).
How
Strength increases with more turns, more current, and a soft-iron core. The field inside a solenoid is strong and uniform.
Where
Scrapyard cranes, electric bells, relays, loudspeakers, MRI machines, maglev trains.
Exam
"Solenoid acts like a bar magnet." "Electromagnet core = soft iron (temporary)." Strength ↑ with turns & current.
Why a scrapyard crane uses an electromagnetSwitch the current on and it grips tonnes of scrap iron; switch it off and the load drops instantly. A permanent magnet could never release its load on command — that's the power of a soft-iron electromagnet.

➡️4 · Force on a Current-carrying Conductor

What
A current-carrying wire placed in a magnetic field feels a force: F = BIL (maximum when the wire is perpendicular to the field).
Why there's a force
The wire's own magnetic field interacts with the external field — the two add on one side and cancel on the other, giving a net sideways push.
How
Fleming's left-hand rule: thuMb = Motion/force, First finger = Field, seCond finger = Current. Force is perpendicular to both field and current.
Where
Electric motors, loudspeakers, moving-coil galvanometers and analogue ammeters.
Exam
"F = BIL." "Fleming's LEFT-hand rule = motor (force)." "Force is zero when the wire is parallel to the field."
Left vs Right handLeft-hand rule → force in a motor (Motor = LM: Left-hand, Motion). Right-hand rule → induced current in a generator. Mixing them is the top trap here.

⚙️5 · Electric Motor

What
A motor turns electrical energy into mechanical (rotation) using the force on a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field.
Why it keeps spinning
After each half turn the coil's sides swap sides of the field, which would reverse the force and stop it. The split-ring commutator reverses the current at that instant, so the turning force always acts the same way.
How
Parts: armature coil, field magnets, split-ring commutator and carbon brushes. Force on the two sides forms a couple → rotation.
Where
Fans, pumps, mixers, washing machines, drills, electric vehicles.
Exam
"Motor: electrical → mechanical." "Commutator reverses current every half turn." Uses Fleming's left-hand rule.

🔋6 · Electromagnetic Induction

What
Faraday's law: a changing magnetic field (flux) through a coil induces an EMF/current. Faster change → larger EMF.
Why motion is essential
A steady field induces nothing — only a change does. Moving a magnet in or out, or spinning a coil, keeps the flux changing and the current flowing.
How
Fleming's right-hand rule gives the induced-current direction. Lenz's law: the induced current always opposes the change that made it (energy conservation).
Where
Generators, transformers, induction cooktops, bicycle dynamos, wireless charging.
Exam
"Faraday: changing flux induces EMF." "Right-hand rule = generator." "Lenz's law: opposes the change (energy conservation)." No motion → no current.

🔌7 · Generator (Dynamo)

What
A generator turns mechanical energy into electrical using electromagnetic induction — the exact reverse of a motor.
Why AC vs DC differ only in the contacts
The coil naturally produces alternating current. Two slip rings pass it out as AC; a split-ring commutator flips it every half turn to give one-way DC.
How
Rotate a coil in a magnetic field → changing flux → induced EMF. Faster rotation, more turns or a stronger field → more voltage.
Where
All power stations (hydro, thermal, nuclear, wind), bicycle dynamos, portable generators.
Exam
"Generator: mechanical → electrical." "AC generator = slip rings; DC = commutator." Reverse of a motor.
Everyday — where your electricity comes fromAlmost all mains electricity is made this way: coal, water, wind or nuclear heat spins a turbine, the turbine spins a generator coil in a magnetic field, and induction produces the current that reaches your home.

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Each has been set in real papers. Read the reality line and it dies.
Trap 1 · Like poles repelLike poles repel, unlike poles attract. "Like poles attract" is false.
Trap 2 · Field lines never crossMagnetic field lines never intersect and run N→S outside the magnet. Options saying they cross or go S→N outside are wrong.
Trap 3 · Oersted vs FaradayOersted = current makes a magnetic field. Faraday = changing field makes a current. Don't swap the discoverers.
Trap 4 · Left = motor, Right = generatorFleming's left-hand rule → force in a motor; right-hand rule → induced current in a generator. The most-swapped pair here.
Trap 5 · Soft iron, not steel, for electromagnetsElectromagnets use soft iron (temporary). Steel is for permanent magnets.
Trap 6 · A steady current induces nothingOnly a changing magnetic flux induces an EMF. A steady current or a stationary magnet induces zero.
Trap 7 · Motor vs Generator conversionMotor: electrical → mechanical. Generator: mechanical → electrical. Reversing the direction is a classic error.
Trap 8 · Slip rings vs commutatorAC generator = two slip rings; DC generator/motor = split-ring commutator. Don't mix them.
Trap 9 · Force directionThe force on a wire is perpendicular to both the current and the field — and is zero when the wire is parallel to the field.
Trap 10 · Copper is not magneticOnly iron, cobalt and nickel are strongly attracted. Copper, aluminium and brass are not magnetic.
Trap 11 · Lenz's law = energy conservationThe induced current opposes the change producing it. This is a consequence of the conservation of energy.
Trap 12 · Commutator's jobThe split-ring commutator reverses the current every half turn — it does not increase voltage or store charge.
Assertion–ReasonA: A scrapyard crane uses an electromagnet, not a permanent magnet. R: An electromagnet can be switched off to release the load. → Both true, R explains A.
Assertion–ReasonA: Moving a magnet into a coil produces a current. R: A changing magnetic flux induces an EMF. → Both true, R explains A.
