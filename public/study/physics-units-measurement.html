🌐Why Measurement Comes First

Physics is the science of measurable quantities. Before any law makes sense, you must fix WHAT you measure and in WHAT unit.
Physical quantity→ Number × Unit→ Compare & calculate→ Laws of physics
Anchor ideaA measurement is always a number multiplied by a unit — "5 metres", never just "5". Change the unit and the number changes (5 m = 500 cm) but the physical length is the same. That single idea drives unit conversion, prefixes and dimensions.

🔢1 · Physical Quantities

What
A physical quantity is anything that can be measured. Two splits matter: fundamental vs derived, and scalar vs vector.
Why the split
You can't define everything from scratch — so scientists chose 7 independent base quantities and build all others from them (speed = length ÷ time). Separately, some quantities need a direction to be meaningful ("50 km/h north"), others don't ("50 kg").
How
Fundamental (7): length, mass, time, current, temperature, amount of substance, luminous intensity. Derived: area, volume, speed, force… Scalar: magnitude only. Vector: magnitude + direction.
Where
Every formula. Recognising a vector (velocity, force, displacement) vs a scalar (speed, distance, mass, energy) is tested directly.
Exam
"Which is a vector?" → velocity/force/displacement. "Which is a scalar?" → speed/mass/energy/distance. "How many fundamental quantities?" → 7.
Classic confusionDistance vs displacement and speed vs velocity: the first of each pair is a scalar, the second a vector. Same number, different category — examiners exploit this constantly.

📐2 · SI Base Units

What
The SI system (Système International, 1960) defines 7 base units that the whole world agrees on.
Why one system
Before SI, every region used its own units (feet, seers, cubits) — trade and science were chaotic. A single agreed set makes results reproducible and comparable anywhere on Earth.
How
The seven: metre (length), kilogram (mass), second (time), ampere (current), kelvin (temperature), mole (amount), candela (luminous intensity).
Where
Science, engineering, medicine, commerce — the base for every derived unit.
Exam
Match unit ↔ quantity (ampere→current, kelvin→temperature, candela→luminous intensity, mole→amount). Symbols after a person's name are capitalised (N, K, A, Pa) but the word is lowercase (newton, kelvin).

🔠3 · Prefixes (Powers of Ten)

What
Prefixes are shorthand for powers of ten attached to a unit — kilometre, milligram, nanosecond.
Why
Writing 0.000000001 s is error-prone. A prefix (1 ns) keeps numbers readable and comparable across the huge range of physics (atoms to galaxies).
How
Multiples: kilo 10³, mega 10⁶, giga 10⁹, tera 10¹². Sub-multiples: milli 10⁻³, micro 10⁻⁶, nano 10⁻⁹, pico 10⁻¹².
Where
Data sizes (GB), wavelengths (nm), chip features (nm), medicine doses (mg, µg), distances (km).
Exam
"nano = ?" → 10⁻⁹. "giga = ?" → 10⁹. "1 µm = ? m" → 10⁻⁶. Watch micro (10⁻⁶) vs milli (10⁻³).
Everyday scaleAtom ≈ 0.1 nm · virus ≈ 100 nm · human hair ≈ 50–100 µm · this text ≈ mm · a walk ≈ km · Earth–Sun ≈ 150 Gm. Prefixes let one system span all of it.

🛠️4 · Measuring Instruments & Least Count

What
Vernier calipers (to 0.1 mm) and the screw gauge / micrometer (to 0.01 mm) measure small lengths precisely. Least count = the smallest value an instrument can read.
Why least count matters
No instrument is infinitely fine. The least count sets the precision limit — you can never report a reading finer than it. A smaller least count means a more precise instrument.
How
Vernier LC = 1 main-scale division − 1 vernier-scale division. Screw gauge LC = pitch ÷ number of circular-scale divisions. Reading = main scale + (coinciding division × LC), corrected for zero error.
Where
Measuring a wire's diameter, a ball bearing, sheet thickness; lab experiments; workshop tools.
Exam
"LC of screw gauge = pitch/divisions." "Which is more precise, vernier or screw gauge?" → screw gauge. "Best tool for a wire's diameter?" → screw gauge.
Zero errorIf an instrument doesn't read zero when closed, every reading is off by a fixed amount — a systematic (zero) error. It must be added/subtracted, and it is the classic example of a systematic (not random) error.

⚙️5 · Derived Units

What
Units built by combining base units — area (m²), speed (m/s), force (N), energy (J), power (W).
Why
Most quantities aren't fundamental. Defining them from the 7 base units keeps the system consistent — a newton is just kg·m/s², nothing new to agree on.
How
Follow the physics: speed = distance/time → m/s. Force = mass × acceleration → kg·m/s² = newton. Work = force × distance → N·m = joule. Power = work/time → J/s = watt.
Where
Electricity bills (kWh), engine power (hp/kW), tyre pressure (Pa/psi), nutrition (kcal/J).
Exam
Named units ↔ quantity: newton→force, pascal→pressure, joule→energy, watt→power, hertz→frequency. "1 kWh = ?" → 3.6×10⁶ J.

🎯6 · Errors & Significant Figures

What
Every measurement has uncertainty. Errors are systematic (constant bias) or random (unpredictable scatter). Significant figures show how many digits are trustworthy.
Why
Reporting "2.4000000 m" from a ruler is dishonest — the ruler can't justify those digits. Sig figs and error analysis communicate how sure you are, which is as important as the value itself.
How
Accuracy = closeness to the true value; precision = closeness of repeated readings. Systematic errors (zero error, wrong calibration) can be corrected; random errors are reduced by averaging many readings.
Where
Lab reports, engineering tolerances, medical tests, quality control.
Exam
"Accuracy vs precision" definition swap. "Zero error is a ___ error" → systematic. "Sig figs in 0.00230" → 3 (leading zeros don't count; trailing zero after a decimal does).
Accuracy vs precision — the targetDarts all in one tight cluster but far from the bull's-eye = precise but not accurate (a systematic error). Scattered around the bull's-eye = accurate but not precise (random error). Tight cluster on the bull's-eye = both.

🧮7 · Dimensions & Dimensional Analysis

What
The dimensional formula expresses any quantity in the base dimensions [M] mass, [L] length, [T] time. Force = [M L T⁻²]; energy = [M L² T⁻²].
Why it's powerful
If an equation is physically correct, both sides must have identical dimensions (you can't add metres to seconds). This lets you check equations, convert units, and derive relationships without doing the full physics.
How
Write each quantity in [M][L][T], substitute, and compare both sides (principle of homogeneity). To convert units, multiply by ratios equal to 1 so units cancel.
Where
Verifying formulas in exams, converting between unit systems, catching algebra mistakes.
Exam
Dimensional formulas of force [M L T⁻²], work/energy [M L² T⁻²], pressure [M L⁻¹ T⁻²], power [M L² T⁻³]. Dimensionless: strain, refractive index, angle, relative density.
Limitations (often asked)Dimensional analysis cannot find pure-number constants (like ½ in ½mv² or 2π), cannot handle trig/log/exponential terms, and cannot prove an equation is fully correct — only that it is dimensionally consistent.

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Each trap has been set in real papers. Read the reality line and it dies.
Trap 1 · kilogram is base, newton is notThe kilogram is a base SI unit; the newton is derived (kg·m/s²). A common wrong option calls the newton "fundamental."
Trap 2 · micro vs millimilli = 10⁻³, micro = 10⁻⁶. "1 micron = 10⁻³ m" is false — a micron (micrometre) is 10⁻⁶ m.
Trap 3 · Light-year & parsec are DISTANCEBoth measure distance, not time, despite "year" in the name. Parsec ≈ 3.26 light-years.
Trap 4 · Smaller least count = MORE preciseA smaller least count means higher precision. Options claiming "smaller LC → lower precision" are wrong.
Trap 5 · Zero error is SYSTEMATICZero error is a constant bias → a systematic error, never random.
Trap 6 · Accuracy ≠ PrecisionThey are not synonyms. Accuracy = closeness to true value; precision = repeatability. You can be precise yet inaccurate.
Trap 7 · Weight vs MassMass (kg) is the amount of matter, constant everywhere. Weight (newton) is the force of gravity and changes with location. A "spring balance" measures weight; a "beam balance" compares mass.
Trap 8 · Sig figs — leading vs trailing zerosIn 0.00230, leading zeros are not significant but the trailing zero is → 3 sig figs. In 100.0, all four count (decimal shown).
Trap 9 · Which is a vector?Displacement, velocity, force, momentum, acceleration are vectors. Distance, speed, mass, energy, work, time are scalars. "Work is a vector" is false.
Trap 10 · Dimensionless quantitiesStrain, refractive index, angle (radian), relative density and specific gravity have no dimensions. Being asked "which is dimensionless" is common.
Trap 11 · Dimensional analysis limitsIt cannot yield numerical constants (½, 2π) and cannot verify equations with trig/exponential terms. "Dimensional analysis proves an equation fully correct" is false.
Assertion–ReasonA: The light-year is a unit of time. R: Light takes a year to travel it. → A is false (it's distance), R is true.
Assertion–ReasonA: A screw gauge is more precise than vernier calipers. R: Its least count (0.01 mm) is smaller. → Both true, R explains A.
