🌐The Big Picture

Sound is a vibration that needs matter to travel. Follow it: from a vibrating body, through a medium, described by wave properties, reflecting as echoes, into your ear.
Vibration makes sound→ Travels through a medium→ v = fλ describes it→ Reflects (echo) → hearing
Anchor ideaSound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave. "Mechanical" means it needs a medium — no air, no sound. "Longitudinal" means the medium's particles vibrate back and forth along the wave (not across it like light). Every trap in this chapter comes back to these two words.

🎵1 · Production of Sound

What
Sound is produced by a vibrating body. Its vibrations create a longitudinal wave of compressions and rarefactions.
Why longitudinal
As the object vibrates, it pushes nearby particles together (compression) then lets them spread apart (rarefaction). Those particles vibrate along the travel direction, passing the disturbance forward.
How
Compression = high-pressure region; rarefaction = low-pressure region. The wave carries energy, not matter.
Where
Vocal cords, drum skins, guitar strings, tuning forks, a ringing bell.
Exam
"Sound is produced by vibration." "Sound is a longitudinal/mechanical wave." "Compression = high pressure, rarefaction = low pressure."
Longitudinal vs TransverseSound is longitudinal (particles vibrate along the wave). Light and water ripples are transverse (vibration is perpendicular). Don't call sound "transverse".

🌫️2 · Medium & Propagation

What
Sound needs a material medium (solid, liquid or gas) to travel. It cannot travel through a vacuum.
Why solids are fastest
Particles in a solid are packed tightly and strongly bonded, so they pass a vibration to their neighbours almost instantly. In gases the particles are far apart, so sound is slow.
How
Speed order: solid > liquid > gas (steel ~5000 m/s, water ~1500, air ~340). Warmer air → faster sound (~0.6 m/s per °C).
Where
Hearing a train by ear on the rail before through air, whale songs travelling far in water, silent space.
Exam
"Sound cannot travel in a vacuum." "Fastest in solids." "Astronauts use radios on the Moon." Speed rises with temperature.
Why space is silentSpace is a near-perfect vacuum — almost no particles. With nothing to pass the vibration along, sound simply cannot travel. Light (an electromagnetic wave) needs no medium, so we can see the stars but never hear them.

📶3 · Wave Properties

What
A sound wave is described by frequency (pitch), amplitude (loudness), wavelength, time period and speed.
Why pitch ≠ loudness
Pitch is about how fast it vibrates (frequency); loudness is about how big the vibration is (amplitude). A soft high note and a loud low note prove they're independent.
How
Frequency (Hz) → pitch; amplitude → loudness (in decibels); wavelength = distance between compressions; T = 1/f.
Where
Male vs female voices (pitch), whisper vs shout (loudness), telling a flute from a violin (timbre).
Exam
"Pitch depends on frequency." "Loudness depends on amplitude." "Timbre distinguishes instruments." Loudness unit = decibel (dB).

🧮4 · The Wave Equation (v = fλ)

What
The one equation linking a wave's speed, frequency and wavelength: v = f × λ.
Why it holds
In one second, f whole waves pass a point, each of length λ — so the wave advances f×λ metres per second. That distance per second is exactly the speed.
How
Rearrange as needed: f = v/λ, λ = v/f. Time period T = 1/f. When sound moves to a new medium its speed and wavelength change but frequency stays the same.
Where
Tuning instruments, radio & musical acoustics, calculating any wave quantity from the other two.
Exam
"v = fλ." "T = 1/f." "Frequency is unchanged when a wave enters a new medium." Numerical staple.

📢5 · Speed of Sound & Echo

What
Sound travels at about 343 m/s in air. An echo is a reflected sound heard as a separate sound after at least 0.1 s.
Why 17 m minimum
Our ears separate two sounds only if they're ≥0.1 s apart. In 0.1 s sound travels ~34 m — there and back — so the obstacle must be at least ~17 m away for a distinct echo.
How
For echo/SONAR: the sound travels to the obstacle and back, so distance = ½ × speed × time.
Where
Echoes in valleys & empty halls, SONAR mapping the sea floor, bats/dolphins locating prey.
Exam
"Speed of sound in air ~340 m/s." "Echo needs ≥17 m." "SONAR: distance = ½vt." "See lightning before thunder (light faster)."
Why lightning then thunderBoth start together, but light travels at 3×10⁸ m/s (nearly instant) while sound crawls at ~340 m/s. The gap in seconds × 340 gives the storm's distance — count "one-one-thousand" and multiply.

🪞6 · Reflection of Sound

What
Sound reflects off surfaces obeying the laws of reflection. Multiple reflections give echoes and reverberation.
Why halls echo
Hard walls reflect sound many times, so it lingers as reverberation. Soft materials (curtains, carpets, foam) absorb it, which is why studios and cinemas are padded.
How
Angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Megaphones, horns, soundboards and whispering galleries all use multiple reflection to direct or focus sound.
Where
Megaphone, stethoscope, bulldog-horn speakers, concert-hall curved ceilings, whispering galleries.
Exam
"Megaphone/stethoscope → multiple reflection." "Reverberation reduced by soft absorbers." Echo is a single distinct reflection; reverberation is many overlapping ones.
Echo vs ReverberationEcho = one clearly separated reflected sound (needs ≥17 m). Reverberation = many quick overlapping reflections that make sound persist in a hall. Don't confuse them.

👂7 · Hearing & Ultrasound

What
Humans hear roughly 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below is infrasound; above is ultrasound.
Why ultrasound is so useful
Its very short wavelength lets it travel in straight beams and reflect off small objects — perfect for imaging and detection without harmful radiation.
How
Infrasound (<20 Hz): whales, elephants, earthquakes. Ultrasound (>20,000 Hz): bats, dolphins, and human technology.
Where
Medical scans (foetus, heart, organs), SONAR, cleaning jewellery/instruments, detecting cracks in metal, breaking kidney stones.
Exam
"Audible range 20 Hz–20 kHz." "Ultrasound > 20 kHz." "Bats/dolphins use ultrasound." "Ultrasound used in scans & SONAR."
Everyday — the ultrasound scanA probe sends ultrasonic pulses into the body; different tissues reflect them differently, and a computer builds an image from the echoes. Because it uses sound, not X-rays, it's safe for scanning a baby.

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Each has been set in real papers. Read the reality line and it dies.
Trap 1 · Sound is longitudinal, not transverseIn sound the particles vibrate along the wave. Only light and ripples are transverse.
Trap 2 · Sound needs a mediumSound cannot travel through a vacuum. Light can. This is why space is silent.
Trap 3 · Fastest in solids, slowest in gasesSpeed order is solid > liquid > gas. "Sound is fastest in air/gas" is false.
Trap 4 · Pitch vs LoudnessPitch depends on frequency; loudness depends on amplitude. Swapping these is the top trap here.
Trap 5 · Frequency stays constant across mediaWhen sound enters a new medium, its speed and wavelength change but its frequency is unchanged.
Trap 6 · Speed of sound rises with temperatureSound travels faster in warmer air (~0.6 m/s per °C), not slower.
Trap 7 · Echo vs ReverberationAn echo is one distinct reflection (needs ≥17 m). Reverberation is many overlapping reflections in a hall.
Trap 8 · Echo distance formulaBecause sound goes to the obstacle and back, distance = ½ × v × t, not v × t.
Trap 9 · Infrasound vs UltrasoundInfrasound < 20 Hz; ultrasound > 20,000 Hz. Don't reverse them.
Trap 10 · Bats use ultrasoundBats and dolphins navigate with ultrasound (echolocation), not infrasound.
Trap 11 · Ultrasound ≠ power sourceUltrasound is used for scans, SONAR, cleaning and flaw detection — not to generate electricity.
Trap 12 · Particles don't travel with the waveThe medium's particles only vibrate about fixed positions; they pass on energy but don't move along with the sound.
Assertion–ReasonA: We hear a distant train sooner by pressing an ear to the rail. R: Sound travels faster in solids than in air. → Both true, R explains A.
Assertion–ReasonA: Sound cannot be heard on the Moon. R: The Moon has no atmosphere to carry sound. → Both true, R explains A.
