🌐How Light Behaves — The Big Map

Every question in this chapter is one of four behaviours. Fix this map first; the rest are details.
Straight line (rectilinear)→ Bounces = Reflection→ Bends = Refraction→ Splits = Dispersion
Anchor factLight is an electromagnetic wave, needs no medium (travels through vacuum), and moves at c = 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum — the universe's speed limit. In any material it slows down; that slowing is the single cause of every bending effect in this chapter.

🪞1 · Reflection & Plane Mirrors

What
Reflection is light bouncing off a surface. Two laws govern it (angles always measured from the normal — the perpendicular to the surface, never from the surface itself).
Why
Light hits atoms in the surface and is re-emitted. On a smooth surface all rays keep the same tilt → a clear image (regular reflection). On a rough surface each tiny facet faces a different way → rays scatter (diffuse reflection). Both obey ∠i = ∠r — the surface roughness, not the law, decides whether you see an image.
How
Law 1: incident ray, reflected ray and normal lie in one plane. Law 2: ∠i = ∠r.
Where
Mirrors, periscopes, kaleidoscopes, why you can read this screen, why paper (rough) doesn't act like a mirror.
Exam
"Angles are measured from the ___" → normal. "A ray hits a mirror at 30° to the surface, find ∠r" → angle from normal = 90−30 = 60°. This surface-vs-normal switch is the #1 trap.
∠i = ∠r, both from the normal (dashed). All three lie in one plane.
Why does a plane-mirror image look reversed?It isn't up-down flipped — it is front-back reversed, which your brain reads as left-right. That is lateral inversion: your right hand looks like the image's left. This is why AMBULANCE is written mirrored on the front of the vehicle so a driver reading it in their rear-view mirror sees it correctly.
EverydayPlane-mirror image is virtual, erect, same size, and stands as far behind the mirror as you are in front. Step back 1 m and your image moves back 1 m — so the gap grows by 2 m. That "×2" is a favourite numerical.

🥄2 · Spherical Mirrors (Concave & Convex)

What
A curved mirror cut from a sphere. Concave = reflecting inner surface (converging). Convex = reflecting outer surface (diverging).
Why
A concave mirror's shape makes every parallel ray tilt inward and cross at one point — the focus (F). A convex mirror tilts them outward, so they only appear to come from a focus behind the mirror. This single geometric fact decides every image they form.
How
f = R/2 — focal length is half the radius of curvature. Image type depends only on where the object sits relative to F and C.
Where
Concave: shaving/make-up mirror (magnified erect image when close), dentist's mirror, torch & headlight reflectors, solar cookers, telescopes. Convex: vehicle rear-view & blind-spot mirrors, shop security mirrors, street corners.
Exam
"Which mirror is used as a rear-view mirror?" → convex (wider field, always small erect image). "Which is used by dentists / in solar cookers?" → concave. "f = ?" → R/2.
Why convex for rear-view?A convex mirror always gives a small, erect, virtual image → it squeezes a wide area into a small glass, giving the driver a large field of view. The trade-off: objects look farther than they are — hence "objects in mirror are closer than they appear."
Why concave for a torch/headlight?Put the bulb at the focus of a concave reflector and every ray comes out parallel → a strong, straight beam that travels far without spreading. Run it backwards (parallel Sun rays in) and they all meet at F → intense heat → solar cooker.
Concave mirror: parallel rays converge at the focus F, midway to the centre C.
MemoryConcave = CAve — curves inward like a cave, and collects/converges light. Convex bulges out and diverges (v for "vehicle" mirror).

🧮3 · Mirror Formula & Sign Convention

What
1/v + 1/u = 1/f links image distance (v), object distance (u) and focal length (f). Magnification m = −v/u = h′/h.
Why signs matter
The formula only works with the New Cartesian sign convention: pole is the origin, distances measured against the incoming light are negative. Get a sign wrong and a real image comes out virtual. The sign of the answer tells you the physics — that is why examiners love it.
How to read the answer
v negative → image in front → real & inverted. v positive → behind mirror → virtual & erect. m negative → inverted (real). m positive → erect (virtual). |m|>1 magnified, <1 diminished.
Where
Numerical questions in every exam; designing camera/telescope optics.
Exam
Sign rules: for a concave mirror f is negative; for a convex mirror f is positive. Object distance u is always negative (object is in front). Forgetting the minus in m = −v/u is the classic slip.

Convex mirror — memorise onceA convex mirror gives only ONE kind of image for every object position: virtual, erect, diminished. No cases to learn. Same is true of a plane mirror (virtual, erect, same size).

🌈4 · Refraction — Why Light Bends

What
Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium into another because its speed changes.
Why does it bend?
Picture a marching band walking from a road onto mud at an angle: the wheel that hits the mud first slows first, so the whole line pivots. Light is the same — the edge entering the denser (slower) medium first makes the beam turn toward the normal. No speed change ⇒ no bend (that's why a ray hitting at 90°/along the normal goes straight through).
How
Snell's law: n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂. Refractive index n = c/v (speed in vacuum ÷ speed in medium). Rule: entering a denser medium → bends toward normal; into a rarer medium → bends away.
Where
A straw looks bent/broken in water; a pool looks shallower than it is; a coin in a bowl reappears when water is poured; stars twinkle; the Sun is visible a few minutes before it actually rises.
Exam
"n = c/v", "diamond has the highest n (2.42) → most sparkle", "which bends more, red or violet? → violet (slower in glass)". Refractive index has no unit (ratio of two speeds) — frequently asked.

Why does a straw look broken?Light from the underwater part bends away from the normal as it leaves water into air, so your eye traces it back along a straighter line — the submerged part appears raised and shifted. Same reason a pond looks shallower (real depth = apparent depth × n).

✨5 · Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

What
When light in a denser medium hits the boundary with a rarer medium at an angle beyond the critical angle, it is 100% reflected back — no light escapes.
Why
Going denser→rarer, light bends away from the normal. Increase the angle and the refracted ray tilts more and more until, at the critical angle (θc), it grazes the surface at 90°. Push past θc and refraction is impossible — the surface acts as a perfect mirror.
How
Two conditions: (1) light travels denser → rarer, (2) angle of incidence > critical angle. For water θc ≈ 48°, glass ≈ 42°, diamond ≈ 24° (small θc → easy TIR → brilliance).
Where
Optical fibres (internet, endoscopy), diamond's sparkle, a mirage on hot roads, sparkle of a prism binocular, shining of a crack in glass.
Exam
"Optical fibre works on ___" → total internal reflection. "Mirage is due to ___" → refraction + TIR in layers of hot air. "Diamond sparkles because of ___" → TIR (small critical angle).
Everyday — mirageOn a hot day, air near the road is hotter (rarer) than air above. Light from the sky bends progressively, undergoes TIR, and reaches your eye as if reflected off water — you "see" a puddle that vanishes as you approach.

🌈6 · Dispersion & Sky Colours

What
Dispersion = splitting of white light into its seven colours (VIBGYOR) by a prism.
Why
White light is a mix of colours. In glass each colour travels at a slightly different speed, so each bends by a different amount. Violet slows most → bends most; red slows least → bends least. They fan out into a spectrum.
How a rainbow forms
Raindrops act as tiny prisms: sunlight refracts in → reflects (TIR) inside → refracts out, dispersing into a bow. The Sun must be behind you. Red on top, violet on the bottom of a primary rainbow.
Where
Rainbow, CD/soap-bubble colours, blue sky, red sunrise/sunset, prism experiments.
Exam
"Colour bending most?" → Violet. "Colour with longest wavelength / bends least?" → Red. "Order of spectrum?" → VIBGYOR. Newton did the prism experiment (1666).
Why is the sky blue but sunset red? (Rayleigh scattering)Air molecules scatter short wavelengths (blue/violet) far more than long ones (∝ 1/λ⁴). By day we see scattered blue from all over the sky (violet is scattered even more but our eyes are less sensitive to it and much is absorbed high up). At sunset light skims a long path through the atmosphere; blue is scattered away, leaving the transmitted red/orange to reach us. Same reason danger signals are red — red scatters least, so it penetrates fog and is seen from farthest.
Why do stars twinkle but planets don't?Stars are point sources; their light passes through shifting air layers of varying density → constant tiny refractions → twinkling. Planets are nearer and appear as small discs (many points), whose flickers average out → steady light.

🔍7 · Lenses & Lens Formula

What
A lens bends light using refraction at two curved surfaces. Convex (converging) is thick in the middle; concave (diverging) is thin in the middle.
Why
A convex lens bends every ray toward a focus because the thicker centre slows light more than the thin edges, curving the wavefront inward. A concave lens does the reverse and spreads light out.
How
Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f (note the minus, unlike mirrors). Power P = 1/f (in metres), unit dioptre (D). Convex power is positive, concave negative. Powers of lenses in contact simply add.
Where
Spectacles, camera, microscope, telescope, magnifying glass, projector, the eye's own lens.
Exam
"Unit of power of a lens?" → dioptre. "A +2 D and −3 D lens in contact → net power?" → −1 D. "Magnifying glass is a ___" → convex lens (object within focus → virtual, erect, magnified).
Mirror vs Lens formula — don't mix themMirror: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f (PLUS). Lens: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f (MINUS). Swapping the sign is the most common exam mistake.

👁️8 · The Human Eye & Its Defects

What
A living convex-lens camera. The cornea does most bending; the lens fine-tunes focus; the image falls on the retina — real, inverted, diminished (the brain flips it upright).
Why we can focus near & far
Accommodation: ciliary muscles change the lens's focal length — fatter for near objects, thinner for distant ones. Iris adjusts the pupil to control light entering, like a camera aperture.
How defects arise
If the image forms in front of the retina → myopia (short-sight). Behind the retina → hypermetropia (long-sight). Correct by adding the right lens power.
Where
Spectacle prescriptions, night-blindness (vitamin A), cataract surgery.
Exam
Near point = 25 cm; far point = infinity. Retina has rods (dim light, B/W) & cones (bright light, colour). Persistence of vision ≈ 1/16 s → cinema. Blind spot = no receptors.

⚠️Exam Traps & Confusion Pairs

Every trap below has been set in real papers. Read the "reality" line and the trap dies.
Trap 1 · Surface vs NormalAngles of incidence & reflection are measured from the NORMAL, never from the mirror surface. "Ray hits at 30° to the surface" → angle of reflection (from normal) = 90−30 = 60°, not 30°.
Trap 2 · Mirror "+" vs Lens "−"Mirror formula uses plus (1/v + 1/u = 1/f); lens formula uses minus (1/v − 1/u = 1/f). Swapping them is the most common numerical error.
Trap 3 · Rear-view mirrorIt is a convex mirror (wide view, erect image) — not concave. Concave is for shaving, dentists and solar cookers.
Trap 4 · Lens for myopiaMyopia (short-sight) is corrected by a concave (diverging) lens. Convex is for hypermetropia. The words "myopia" and "convex" together = wrong option.
Trap 5 · Refractive index has no unitn = c/v is a ratio of two speeds → dimensionless, no unit. Any option giving it a unit (m/s, etc.) is a distractor.
Trap 6 · Sky blue but Sun redBoth are the same phenomenon — Rayleigh scattering. Sky is blue (scattered short λ); sunset is red (blue scattered away over a long path). Don't call one "reflection."
Trap 7 · Which bends more — red or violet?Violet bends most (shortest wavelength, slowest in glass); red bends least. Reverse for wavelength: red has the longest wavelength.
Trap 8 · Speed of light in a mediumLight is fastest in vacuum/air and slows in denser media. "Light speeds up entering glass" is false. Frequency stays constant; speed & wavelength change.
Trap 9 · Real image is always inverted; virtual is erectA real image can be caught on a screen and is inverted. A magnifier/plane mirror gives a virtual, erect image that can't be projected.
Trap 10 · TIR directionTotal internal reflection happens only denser → rarer (e.g. glass to air), never rarer → denser. Also requires angle > critical angle.
Trap 11 · Mirage vs Rainbow causeMirage = refraction + TIR in hot air. Rainbow = dispersion + internal reflection in raindrops. Don't swap the mechanisms.
Trap 12 · TwinklingStars twinkle, planets don't. Cause is atmospheric refraction, not the star "blinking." Planets are discs, so flicker averages out.
Trap 13 · Power sign & additionConvex lens = + power, concave = −. Lenses in contact: powers add (+2D & −3D → −1D), focal lengths do NOT add directly.
Trap 14 · Focal length relationf = R/2 for mirrors. A common distractor states f = 2R or f = R — both wrong.
Assertion–Reason styleA: A convex mirror is used as a rear-view mirror. R: It always forms an erect, diminished image with a wide field of view. → Both true, R correctly explains A.
Assertion–Reason styleA: The sky appears blue. R: Blue light has the longest wavelength. → A true, R false (blue has a short wavelength; it is scattered more).
